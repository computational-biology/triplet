A Brief Introduction

What is an RNA Triplet

Ribonucleic Acids (RNA) are a single chain nucleic acid sequences. To stebilize its structures, it creates base-base interactions or base pairing. Some times one base interacts or forms base pairs with more than one bases (e.g C=G=A. here the central guanine forms a pair with C and another pair with A). In such cases we call them base triplets.

Why are they biologically important?

Specific interactions between nucleotide bases are extremely important for formation of various well defined structures of the different functional RNA, such as Ribosome, tRNA, etc. These specific interactions take place in the form of base pairing assisted by couple of hydrogen bonds between the two participating bases. Furthermore, base pairing in RNA is not restricted within Watson-Crick type A:U and G:C only but various non-canonical base pairs are quite often observed in the available structures of RNA. Such non-canonical base pairing may involve the Hoogsteen edge of especially the purines (where hydrogen bonds are possible using N7 atoms of the purines as acceptors) or sugar edge of the bases, also involving 2'-OH group of ribose sugars. Either of the bases of these base pairs can further specifically interact with another base of the RNA forming base triplets. Such base triplets or even higher order structures have been observed quite frequently. Non-canonical base pairing is mandatory in these base triplets. The present database attempts to provide several additonal information about the base triplets found in RNA crystal structures.
